Section 1. Statistical Test

1.1 Which statistical test did you use to analyze the NYC subway data? Did you use a one-tail or a two-tail P value? What is the null hypothesis? What is your p-critical value?

        The Mann-Whitney U Test is the appropriate statistical test to analyze the NYC Subway data.   Based on the results of the Mann-Whitney U test, a one tailed P-value was obtained.  The resultant p-value was multiplied by 2 to create a 2-tailed p-value.  Given the fact that this type of test was used, the null hypothesis is as follows: There is no statistical difference between the distribution of entries on both rainy and non-rainy days.  The NYC subway data set yielded a 1-tailed p-value of 0.025 & 2-sided p-value of 0.05.

1.2 Why is this statistical test applicable to the dataset? In particular, consider the assumptions that the test is making about the distribution of ridership in the two samples.

        The Mann-Whitney U test is the appropriate statistical test for this dataset for two reasons.  First,  as the distribution of ridership did not follow any particular underlying probability distribution (i.e normal distribution), a nonparametric test must be used.  Second, a test that distinguishes two population (i.e distributions of entries on both rainy & non-rainy days) from each other statistically is necessary.

1.3 What results did you get from this statistical test? These should include the following numerical values: p-values, as well as the means for each of the two samples under test.

        The following results were obtained:

        U-value = 1924409167.0

        P-value: 0.05 (Two-sided)

        The mean entries into Subway without rain: 1090.28

        The mean entries into Subway with rain: 1105.45

1.4 What is the significance and interpretation of these results?

        As the P-value of the two-tailed test is 0.05, it can be said that with 5% confidence, the null hypothesis can be rejected and there is a significant statistical difference between the distribution of NYC subway ridership on rainy and non-rainy days.

Based on these results, it can stated with 5% confidence that there is a significant statistical difference difference in the average number of subway entries per hour depending on if it is raining or not;  thus we reject the null hypothesis.
